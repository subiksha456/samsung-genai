Unit 1
Exploring Diverse Foundation Models
One base model, trained once, reused for hundreds of downstream tasks.

Stanford HAI definition

A foundation model is trained on broad data at scale, using self-supervised learning, so it can be adapted (fine-tuned or prompted) to a wide range of downstream tasks — rather than being built from scratch for each one. GPT, Claude, and Gemini are all foundation models; a spam classifier trained only on spam emails is not.

Pre-training vs. Fine-tuning

Pre-training — one expensive pass over a massive, broad dataset (a large chunk of the public internet), producing a general-purpose base model. Fine-tuning — a much cheaper, smaller second pass on task-specific data, adapting that base model to a narrower job (e.g. a legal-contract reviewer).

Self-Supervised Learning

The model creates its own training labels from the raw data itself — e.g. "predict the next word" needs no human to label anything. This is why foundation models can train on the scale of the internet: there's no manual labelling bottleneck.

Emergent Abilities & Scaling Laws

Scaling laws: as you add more parameters, data, and compute, a model's loss (its error rate on the training objective) decreases smoothly and predictably — you can plot it as a clean curve.

Emergent abilities are the strange exception: some capabilities — multi-step arithmetic, translating between low-resource languages, explaining a joke — don't appear gradually. Below a certain scale threshold the model simply cannot do the task at all; above it, performance jumps. You cannot predict emergent abilities by extrapolating the smooth loss curve — they show up as a genuine surprise, discovered only after training a bigger model and testing it.

In-Class Game Scale It Up
▶
⏱ 12 min · 8–10 volunteers form a growing line, rest of room votes
📈 Primer: a scaling law is a smooth, predictable curve — bigger model, gradually better. An emergent ability is different — a specific skill that is simply absent below a size threshold and suddenly present above it, not a gradual improvement. Today's game makes that "sudden jump" physical instead of abstract.

8–10 volunteers line up at the front, each holding a card with a parameter count (125M, 350M, 1.3B, 6B, 13B, 70B, 175B, 540B — smallest to largest). Trainer keeps a running "smooth loss curve" sketched on the whiteboard throughout, dropping a little with each new volunteer added — that part stays gradual on purpose.

  • Trainer reveals one task per round (e.g. "explain why this joke is funny," "do 3-digit multiplication," "translate a proverb between two low-resource languages")
  • Starting from the smallest card, add one volunteer to the line at a time. At each size, the room shouts a guess: can a model this size do the task?
  • Below the threshold volunteer, that "model" gives a scripted blank/wrong non-answer (trainer preps this in advance per task) — no matter how much bigger it gets, still wrong, still wrong…
  • Then one specific volunteer (the threshold size, chosen in advance per task) suddenly performs the task correctly and confidently — a visible jump, not a gradual improvement
  • Point back at the whiteboard's smooth loss curve: it kept improving gradually the whole time, but the actual skill only appeared at one specific point
💡 Debrief: this is exactly why "just make it bigger" is a real strategy, not lazy engineering — some capabilities are only unlocked at scale, and nobody can fully predict which ones or exactly where, until someone trains the bigger model and tests it.
🧠 A colleague says "a bigger foundation model is just a smoother, marginally better version of a smaller one." What's wrong with that claim?
It's only half true. Loss/accuracy does improve smoothly with scale (the scaling law part) — but some specific abilities appear suddenly at a size threshold rather than gradually (emergent abilities). A model can be "10% bigger" and go from completely unable to do a task, to reliably able to do it — that's not a smooth improvement, it's a step change, and it's part of why model selection isn't just "pick the cheapest one that's big enough."
Unit 2
Task-Specific Foundation Models
Not every foundation model does everything — and knowing the category tells you what to expect.

Unimodal vs. Multimodal

Unimodal — handles one type of input/output (text-only, or image-only). Multimodal — natively handles multiple types together (text + image + audio in a single conversation), e.g. describing a photo you upload, or reasoning over a PDF's charts and text at once.

Reasoning vs. Creativity

Text/code models are generally optimised for reasoning — correctness, logic, following instructions precisely. Image/video/audio generation models are optimised for creativity — plausible, aesthetically strong output, where there's rarely one "correct" answer. Different objective, different evaluation, different model.

Unit 3
Industry-Specific Foundation Models
General-purpose vs. Vertical AI — and why enterprises often pay for both.

General-purpose foundation models (GPT, Claude, Gemini) are broad by design — good at almost everything, expert at nothing specific. Vertical AI models are fine-tuned or built on proprietary, domain-specific data for one industry — narrower scope, but far higher reliability inside that domain, because the model has actually seen the terminology, edge cases, and regulatory language that matters there.

💰

Finance

Fraud detection, algorithmic research, regulatory filing analysis — trained on market and compliance data general models never see in depth.

⚖️

Legal

Contract review, case-law research — needs precise citation and jurisdiction awareness that a general model will confidently get wrong (see Day 1's hallucination unit).

🏥

Healthcare

Radiology image analysis, clinical note summarisation — highest-stakes domain, heaviest regulatory and explainability requirements (previews Day 4).

🏭

Manufacturing

Defect detection from camera feeds, predictive maintenance from sensor data — Samsung's own production lines use models like this today.

🔬

Science

Research acceleration, protein-structure prediction (e.g. AlphaFold) — models trained on domain data no general web-scrape ever included.

🏢

Enterprise-specific

A company's own internal model, fine-tuned on its own proprietary documents — the most specific tier, and the most expensive to build and maintain.

Domain adaptation — how a general model becomes a vertical one

Start from a strong general-purpose foundation model, then fine-tune it on a curated, proprietary dataset from the target domain (anonymised patient notes, internal legal precedent, historical fraud cases). The base model's broad language ability is retained; the fine-tuning teaches it the domain's specific vocabulary, edge cases, and correct-answer patterns.

Unit 4
Foundation Model Architecture
Transformer and diffusion internals, recapped through the lens of scale.

Attention, at a high level

Every token in a prompt can "look at" every other token and weigh how relevant it is before producing the next word — this is what lets a model connect "it" on page 3 back to a noun on page 1. More parameters generally means more attention heads, watching for more distinct kinds of relationships at once.

Context Window

The maximum amount of text (measured in tokens) a model can "see" at once — prompt plus conversation history plus any attached documents. Runs out mid-conversation and the model literally cannot see the earliest messages anymore, regardless of how relevant they were.

Unit 5
Open Source vs. Closed Models
Neither side wins every scenario — that's the actual lesson.
In-Class Game Pick a Side
▶
⏱ 12 min · Room splits in half, debate format
⚖️ Primer: open-weight models (Llama, Mistral) can be downloaded, self-hosted, and fully customised — nothing leaves your servers. Closed models (GPT, Claude, Gemini) are accessed only via API — you don't see or control the weights, but you get the provider's latest capability with zero infrastructure to manage. Neither is universally "better" — it depends on the scenario.

Split the room down the middle: left half argues for Open-Weight, right half argues for Closed/Proprietary — no matter their personal opinion, they must argue their assigned side. Trainer reads one business scenario per round.

  • Trainer reads a scenario aloud (e.g. "A hospital wants AI reading X-rays, but patient data legally cannot leave the building")
  • Each side gets 30 seconds to shout out their single strongest argument for that specific scenario
  • Room (both sides together) votes by show of hands on which side had the stronger case — for this scenario only
  • Repeat with 2–3 more scenarios, deliberately chosen so the "winning" side flips each time (e.g. next: "A startup needs the best possible coding assistant today, has no infra team, and speed to market matters more than anything")
💡 Debrief: point out that the winning side changed every round — that flip is the lesson. "Open vs. closed" isn't a contest with a permanent winner; it's a trade-off you re-evaluate per use case, which is exactly what Section 9's Model Selection Strategy formalises.
🧠 A startup's CTO says "we should always use open-weight models — they're free and we can self-host." What's the flaw in "always"?
Self-hosting is only "free" if you ignore GPU infrastructure, ops staff, and the engineering time to keep pace with closed-model providers' constant capability upgrades. For a small team without infra expertise that needs the current best-in-class reasoning fast, a closed API is often actually cheaper and faster to ship with — "always" is the tell that this is a rule-of-thumb being applied without checking the actual scenario.

🧠 A startup building an MVP and a regulated bank rebuilding its core codebase both need a coding assistant. Should they pick the same model?
Almost certainly not. The startup optimises for speed and cost — a fast, cheap closed API is likely the right call. The bank optimises for security, auditability, and compliance — it may need an on-prem or private-cloud deployment, tighter data controls, and a model whose behaviour can be logged and explained to a regulator. Same task category (coding), completely different constraint set, completely different right answer.
Unit 9
Enterprise Considerations
What changes once a model moves from "cool demo" to "production system."

Security & Governance

Who can call the model, with what data, and who reviews the output before it reaches a customer? Enterprise deployments need access controls and audit trails, not just an API key shared in a chat.

Compliance & Deployment Choice

Regulated industries (finance, healthcare) often can't use a public cloud API at all — deployment choice (cloud vs. private cloud vs. fully on-prem) is frequently decided by legal/compliance before any model-quality comparison even starts.

Domain Adaptation

Same idea as Unit 3's vertical AI, but as an ongoing enterprise process — continuously fine-tuning on the company's own proprietary data as it accumulates, not a one-time setup step.

Responsible AI & Model Evaluation

Before any model ships, enterprises run structured evaluation — accuracy benchmarks, bias testing, hallucination rate checks. This is exactly what Lab 1 (Interrogation Room) has you do by hand, at a much smaller scale, today.
