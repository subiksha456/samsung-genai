Unit 1
Understanding AI Ethics
Not a compliance checkbox — a business strategy, and here's the incident that made that obvious to everyone at once.

The moment this stopped being theoretical

In 2023, an AI-generated image of Pope Francis in a white puffer jacket spread across social media — millions believed it was real before it was debunked. No malicious intent, no targeted attack, just a diffusion model doing exactly what it was built to do: generate a plausible image from a text prompt. That single, harmless-seeming image is now a standard teaching case for why "the technology worked correctly" and "nothing went wrong" are not the same statement.

IBM

AI ethics = a set of guidelines addressing how AI systems should be designed and used to avoid unintended, harmful outcomes and align with human values.

UNESCO

Frames AI ethics around human rights and dignity — fairness, non-discrimination, and human oversight are treated as baseline requirements, not optional extras.

TechTarget

A practical/operational lens: the policies, processes, and technical guardrails an organisation puts in place to govern how it actually builds and deploys AI.

Why "ethics as strategy" beats "ethics as compliance"

A compliance mindset asks "what's the minimum required so we don't get sued?" A strategy mindset asks "what builds enough trust that customers, regulators, and our own employees keep choosing to use this system?" Trust is now a competitive differentiator — a company known for careless AI deployments loses customers even where no law was technically broken. The rest of today is about building the second mindset, not just the first.

🧠 Someone on your team says "we followed every regulation, so we're covered on AI ethics." What's missing from that claim?
Regulation is a floor, not a ceiling — it defines the minimum legally required behaviour, usually written after specific incidents already happened elsewhere. Trust, reputation, and user harm can all be damaged by something perfectly legal (e.g. a biased hiring model that hasn't yet been outlawed in that jurisdiction). "Compliant" and "ethical" overlap heavily but are not identical claims — today's content is really about the wider circle, not just the legal minimum.

🧠 A model gives correct, unbiased answers but nobody at the company can explain *how* it reached them. Which principle is at risk, even though Fairness and Reliability are both fine?
Explainability (and closely related, Transparency). A model can be accurate and unbiased today and still be a serious risk if nobody can trace why it made a specific decision — which matters enormously the day it makes a wrong or contested one, especially in healthcare, finance, or government, where a decision often has to be justified to a regulator or an affected person.

Privacy & Security
Privacy & Security
The risk that starts the moment an employee pastes something into a chat box.

Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

Names, ID numbers, contact details, financial records, health data — anything that can identify a specific person. Once pasted into a third-party AI tool, you generally lose control over where it's stored, whether it's used for future training, and who else might see it.

Confidential & Enterprise Data

Source code, unreleased product specs, internal financials, customer contracts — the same "just paste it in to save time" instinct that leaks PII also leaks trade secrets. Several real enterprise incidents trace back to exactly this.

Prompt Privacy — the habit to build now

Before pasting anything into an AI tool, ask: would I be comfortable if this exact text appeared in a public screenshot tomorrow? If the answer is no, redact names/IDs/specifics first, or use an enterprise-tier tool with a data-processing agreement that guarantees your input isn't used for training.

AI Security Risks

Beyond accidental leaks: prompt injection (a hidden instruction embedded in a document or webpage that hijacks the model's behaviour), model output used for fraud (the Hong Kong deepfake case from Section 3), and data poisoning (deliberately corrupting training data to bias a model's future outputs). Security for AI systems is a live, evolving field — not a solved problem.

🧠 A colleague pastes a customer's full name, phone number, and complaint history into a public AI chatbot to draft a reply faster. What's the actual risk, even if the drafted reply itself is fine?
The risk isn't the output quality — it's that the customer's PII has now left the company's control and entered a third-party system whose storage, retention, and training-data policies your colleague almost certainly hasn't checked. The reply being good doesn't undo the privacy exposure that already happened the moment it was pasted in.
Hallucinations & Reliability
Why LLMs Hallucinate
A quick recap through the governance lens — you saw this happen firsthand in Day 3's Interrogation Room.

An LLM is trained to produce the statistically most plausible next token — not to check whether a claim is true. When it doesn't actually know an answer, it doesn't have a built-in "I don't know" reflex by default; it generates a confident-sounding answer anyway, which is exactly what made the ChatGPT hallucinated case-law incident (Section 3) a real legal liability — not just an embarrassing output, but a federal court sanction.

Factual Verification

Cross-check any AI-generated fact, citation, or policy claim against a real source before it's acted on or repeated to a customer — the same habit Day 3's Hallucination Lab and RAG lab both built.

Human Validation

For any high-stakes output (legal, medical, financial, customer-facing policy), a human reviews before it ships — treating the model's answer as a draft, not a verdict.

Reducing incorrect outputs

Grounding with real documents (RAG, Day 3 Lab 2), lowering "creativity" settings for factual tasks, explicitly instructing the model to say "I don't know" when uncertain, and — the one no technical fix replaces — keeping a human in the loop for anything a wrong answer could actually hurt someone over.

Explainability (XAI)
Black-Box vs. Explainable AI
Can anyone actually say *why* the model decided what it decided?

Why it matters most in healthcare, finance, and government

A denied loan, a rejected insurance claim, or a flagged medical scan all carry a legal or ethical expectation that someone can explain the reasoning to the affected person — "the model said so" is not an acceptable answer to a regulator, a court, or a patient. This is also where the philosophical "Chinese Room" argument gets raised: even if a system produces correct-looking outputs, does it need to "understand" anything for its decisions to be trustworthy, or is behaviour alone enough? Enterprises don't need to resolve the philosophy — but they do need traceability.

AI Governance
Governance Frameworks
The organisational machinery that turns principles into enforced practice.

AI Policies

Written rules on what AI tools can be used for, what data can and can't be entered, and who approves a new AI use case before it launches.

Human-in-the-Loop

A required human checkpoint before a high-stakes AI decision takes effect — not an optional nicety, a designed control point.

AI Lifecycle Management

Governance doesn't stop at launch — models are monitored, re-evaluated, and retired across their full lifecycle as data drifts and new risks surface.

Monitoring, Documentation & Audit Trails

Every significant AI decision is logged — what the model saw, what it output, who reviewed it — so an incident can actually be investigated afterward, not reconstructed from memory.

🧠 A team ships an AI feature with no logging of its inputs/outputs, reasoning "we trust the model, so why log it?" What governance failure is this, independent of whether the model is actually accurate?
Missing monitoring/audit trail. Even a perfectly accurate model needs a paper trail — the day something goes wrong (a complaint, a regulator inquiry, a discrimination claim), "we trusted it" is not a defensible answer without logs showing what actually happened. Audit trails are an insurance policy against exactly the failure modes Section 3's six cases all eventually had to explain to someone.

Responsible AI in Enterprises
From Principle to Practice
What a real enterprise AI approval process actually looks like.

AI Risk Assessment

Before deployment: what's the worst plausible failure, who does it harm, and how likely is it — scored, not guessed.

Ethical Deployment

Staged rollout, clear scope limits, and an explicit "this system should not be used for X" boundary communicated to everyone who touches it.

Governance Committees

A cross-functional group (legal, engineering, ethics, business) that reviews and approves AI use cases before launch — not one engineer's judgement call alone.

Continuous Monitoring

Approval isn't a one-time gate — deployed models are re-checked as usage patterns, data, and regulations all shift over time.
